At First Glance

Information Security: the name is self-explanatory.

This area of research studies how information is protected against misuse. Just like banks put effort into safeguarding their valuables, people need to protect their information because in this day and age, information means money and access.

Information at Risk

Unlike computer security, information security handles both virtual and physical forms of information

An ID card is a physical form of user information that could enable someone to impersonate an employee and gain access to sensitive information. Meanwhile, email passwords are the key to receiving verification codes and can be used to compromise accounts linked to your email.

Researching Information Security

Information security research studies the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information in order to protect it from misuse. Information must be confidential such that access is restricted to intended recipients and users. In addition, it must also maintain its integrity, so that it is not corrupted or misconstrued. Finally, information must have availability so that security practices do not infringe on its ability to be transferred and used.

The importance of research in this field is twofold: there have been mounting challenges to information security as larger quantities of information are being stored, accessed, and shared in a digital world. In addition, there is a lack of education and awareness of the importance of information security.

The scope of information security research ranges from surgically specific ---analyzing a specific security mechanism for cryptocurrency--- to prolifically applicable, like analyzing how people are manipulated by social engineering. It can go from abstract --- mathematical algorithms for cryptography --- to real world applications, like how investment banks use financial cryptography.

Research Starter Pack

Three rather notable areas of Information Security, summarized succinctly and designed to jumpstart your understanding of the field


Social Engingeering
In Season 3, Episode 3 of BBC’s Sherlock, Sherlock Holmes uses social engineering in order to gather confidential information. Sherlock needed to break into a certain man’s office, so he dated that man’s secretary for a time and established a strong relationship with her. He eventually used that relationship to worm his way into the securely locked building and attempted to steal confidential computer files.

In the context of information security, social engineering is the act of manipulating human aspects of a system into giving access to sensitive information. Sherlock’s move was planned very far ahead but many types of social engineering can be spontaneous and often rely on an abrupt effect. Other social engineering techniques include:

  • phishing emails: sending links in emails from a supposedly trustworthy source and installing malware upon activating the link
  • tailgating: something as simple as walking behind someone who has legitimate ID in an area that checks for identification, like an electronically locked door
  • baiting: loading a device with malware and leaving it somewhere where someone will inevitably find it and try to check its contents by plugging it into a secure computer---thereby installing the malware onto it

Cyrptography

In Season 6, Episode 5 of The Good Wife, law partner Diane Lockhart clicks on a popup and inadvertently installs ransomware onto the entire network of computers in her law firm, locking their information and files. As she is on a deadline for a trial, she must pay $50,000 to regain access to her computer systems.

Ransomware, a program that locks information and holds it captive for ransom until payment is given, is one of the most frustrating types of malware. Malware, short for malicious software, is “any file or software that is harmful to a computer user.”

In addition to ransomware, there is also spyware, which tracks a user’s activity/information, and worms, which are independent self-replicating malware programs. Intuitively speaking, malware is any kind of virus you can imagine and often is combatted with firewalls and anti-malware software.

Cyrptography

Though ciphers and codes have been used for thousands of years, the struggle to unlock the Enigma Code was so inspiring that it was made into a movie. The Imitation Game portrays the brilliant computer scientist Alan Turing as he worked to build a machine that would decrypt the German’s Enigma Code, a WWII cipher that changed daily.

Cryptography is at the very essence of information security and is the primary mechanism used to protect user information and authenticate identity. Sub-fields of cryptography include analyzing cryptographic methods, branches of cryptography that hide information in plain sight, and financial cryptography. From passwords to passcodes to Touch ID and voice recognition, cryptography permeates every aspect of information security.
